# Ai Prompting For Unfamiliar Domains

## Core Rules

- Always maintain the same conversation thread to benefit from compounding context across all three phases.
- Utilize the highest-end reasoning model available to ensure deep insight and logical consistency.
- Use dictation when providing context to capture rich, unedited detail without self-censorship.
- Request simplified executive briefings (e.g., 5th-grade level) to grasp core concepts quickly without jargon.
- Apply the inversion principle by focusing on how a project could fail rather than just how it could succeed.
- Prioritize second-order effects over immediate, obvious results to understand long-term implications.
- Solicit 'veteran advice'â€”the kind of insights a 20-year expert would share over coffee that aren't found in manuals.
- End every session with a concrete 'cheat sheet' or action plan containing specific questions and first steps.

## When to Apply

- When faced with a project or challenge completely outside your past experience.
- When you need to get up to speed on a new domain's vocabulary and landscape in minutes.
- When preparing for a high-stakes conversation where you are 'smart but not an expert'.
- When you want to identify blind spots and expensive mistakes before they happen.

## Anti-Patterns

- Don't start new chat threads between the Map, Mine, and Move phases; you lose the cumulative context.
- Avoid asking basic, generic questions that result in average, surface-level AI answers.
- Don't focus exclusively on the path to success; ignoring potential failures leads to hidden traps.
- Don't rely on low-tier or faster models for these tasks; they often miss the subtle complexities and second-order effects.

## Workflow

1. Map: Define your role and situation, then request an executive briefing on key terms, major approaches, and common outsider misunderstandings.
2. Mine: Use dictation to provide deep context and ask the AI to uncover hidden complexities, expensive mistakes, and veteran-level insights.
3. Analyze: Specifically request second-order effects and tricky elements that seem simple but are actually difficult.
4. Move: Dictate your final readiness needs and request 3-5 critical questions to ask, information to gather, and the smartest first step.
5. Action: Consolidate all derived insights into a tactical cheat sheet for immediate use.

## Examples

- A business owner learning about marketing agency pricing structures (retainers vs. performance-based) before hiring.
- Identifying 'bait and switch' risks where senior executives pitch a project but juniors actually execute the work.
- Discovering that a new ad account requires 3-4 months of testing before meaningful results are possible.
